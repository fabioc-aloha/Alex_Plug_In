## Purpose

Write content that engages readers: reports that people actually read, emails that get responses, documents that hold attention. Creative writing principles apply far beyond fiction. They make any writing more engaging, memorable, and persuasive.

## Steps

1. Identify the purpose: inform, persuade, entertain, or inspire?
2. Identify the audience: what do they already know? What do they care about?
3. Choose a narrative structure:
   - **Three-act** (setup, conflict, resolution) for reports and proposals
   - **Hero's journey** (challenge, struggle, transformation) for case studies
   - **In medias res** (start in the middle of the action) for attention-grabbing openings
   - **Problem-solution** for recommendations and pitches
4. Write the opening: the first sentence determines whether they keep reading
5. Use concrete details instead of abstractions
6. Vary sentence rhythm: short sentences create urgency. Longer sentences build context and nuance.
7. Draft the full piece, then edit ruthlessly (cut 20-30% on first edit)
8. Create the document in Word

## Opening Techniques

| Technique      | Example                                                | Best for                 |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------ |
| Startling fact | "Half our customers leave within 90 days."             | Reports, proposals       |
| Question       | "What would you do with an extra hour every day?"      | Emails, pitches          |
| Anecdote       | "Last Tuesday, a support ticket changed our strategy." | Case studies             |
| Contrast       | "We expected growth. We got a wake-up call."           | Analysis, retrospectives |

Avoid: "The purpose of this document is to..." (nobody wants to read that)

## Show, Don't Tell

| Telling (weak)               | Showing (strong)                                                           |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| "The project was successful" | "The project delivered $2.3M in savings and finished 2 weeks early"        |
| "The team worked hard"       | "The team shipped 47 features in 6 weeks, averaging 3 deployments per day" |
| "Customers are frustrated"   | "NPS dropped from 72 to 41 in one quarter"                                 |

## Editing Checklist

- [ ] Delete the first paragraph (often throat-clearing; the real start is paragraph 2)
- [ ] Replace every adjective with a specific fact or number
- [ ] Read aloud: cut anything that sounds unnatural
- [ ] Check: does every paragraph advance the argument?
- [ ] Is the opening compelling enough to keep reading?
- [ ] Is the ending a clear call to action or memorable takeaway?

## Output Format

- Word document with structured narrative
- Varied sentence length and engaging opening
- Specific details and numbers replace vague claims

## Guidelines

- Write like you talk, then edit for precision
- Specific is always better than general
- Active voice: "The team delivered" not "The deliverables were completed by the team"
- Cut ruthlessly: if it doesn't serve the story, remove it
- End strong: the last sentence is the second most important after the first
- Paragraphs should be 3-5 sentences max; break up walls of text
