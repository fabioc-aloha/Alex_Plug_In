Business Operations

=== REQUIREMENTS AND ANALYSIS ===

Requirements Hierarchy:
- Business Requirements: what the organization needs ("increase customer retention by 15%")
- Stakeholder Requirements: what users/stakeholders need ("see my ticket status in real time")
- Solution Requirements: what the system must do (functional + non-functional)
- Transition Requirements: what's needed to go from current to future state

SMART Criteria (every requirement must pass):
- Specific: clear, unambiguous statement
- Measurable: quantifiable success criteria
- Achievable: technically and organizationally feasible
- Relevant: traces to a business need
- Time-bound: has a deadline or milestone

Elicitation Techniques:
- Interviews: 1:1 with stakeholders, open-ended questions, follow "tell me about your typical day"
- Workshops: Open > Diverge > Converge > Close pattern, max 8 participants
- Document analysis: existing SOPs, system docs, error logs
- Observation: watch users work, note workarounds they've created
- Prototyping: low-fidelity mockups to validate understanding
- Surveys: broad input, quantifiable patterns, use for prioritization

Use Case Levels:
- Summary: high-level business goal ("customer onboards successfully")
- User Goal: complete task ("customer submits application form")
- Subfunction: supporting step ("system validates email format")

SIPOC Process Template:
- Supplier: who provides input
- Input: what triggers the process
- Process: 5-7 high-level steps
- Output: what the process produces
- Customer: who receives the output

Gap Analysis Framework:
- Current state: documented as-is process
- Desired state: target from requirements
- Gap identification: missing capabilities, blockers, risks
- Gap categories: people (skills, capacity), process (workflow, policy), technology (tools, integration)

Gherkin Acceptance Criteria:
- Given [precondition/context]
- When [action/trigger]
- Then [expected result]
- And [additional conditions]

Traceability Matrix:
Every requirement traces: Business Need > Stakeholder Need > Solution Requirement > Test Case > Deliverable. Orphan requirements (no business trace) are scope creep candidates.

=== PRIORITIZATION ===

MoSCoW Method:
- Must Have (~60% of effort): system fails without these
- Should Have (~20%): important but not critical
- Could Have (~20%): nice-to-have, first to cut
- Won't Have (this time): explicitly deferred, documented for future
Rule: if everything is "Must Have," the prioritization failed

Kano Model:
- Basic (must-be): expected, absence causes dissatisfaction, presence doesn't delight
- Performance (one-dimensional): more is better, linear satisfaction
- Excitement (attractive): unexpected delight, absence doesn't disappoint
- Indifferent: customer doesn't care either way
- Reverse: feature actively annoys some customers

=== SCOPE MANAGEMENT ===

Scope Health Check Metrics:
- Requirements added after kickoff: "added since baseline" count
- Change request frequency: more than 2/sprint signals drift
- Scope-to-timeline ratio: growing scope with fixed deadline = risk
- Stakeholder alignment: can all stakeholders state the same project scope?

Warning Signs:
- "While we're at it, can we also..."
- "This is a small change" (repeated)
- "The customer also mentioned..."
- Requirements growing but timeline stays fixed
- Different stakeholders describe different scopes

5 Scope Negotiation Patterns:
1. Capture and Defer: "Great idea. Adding to backlog for Phase 2."
2. Trade-Off: "We can add X if we remove Y. Which do you prefer?"
3. MVP Challenge: "What's the minimum version that delivers value?"
4. 80/20 Cut: "This 20% of features delivers 80% of value. Let's start there."
5. Parking Lot: "Noted. We'll revisit at the sprint boundary."

=== MEETING EFFICIENCY ===

Meeting Cost Formula:
Cost = (number of attendees) x (average hourly rate) x (duration in hours)
Example: 8 people x $75/hr x 1 hour = $600 per meeting
If the meeting doesn't produce $600+ in value, it shouldn't exist.

Meeting-or-Not Decision:
- Decide something: maybe (async decision doc if simple)
- Share information: rarely (email, video, document instead)
- Brainstorm: often (async + sync hybrid works well)
- Build relationships: yes (no substitute for presence)
- Status updates: no (dashboards, async standup bot instead)

Meeting Categories:
- Decision (30-60 min): options, criteria, designated decider
- Creative (60-90 min): prompt, diverge/converge structure
- Tactical (15-30 min): blockers, handoffs, quick resolution
- Strategic (60-120 min): context, options, trade-offs
- 1:1 (30-60 min): their agenda first, development focus

Agenda Template:
Meeting: [Purpose Statement]
Duration: [X min], Attendees: [Required] + [Optional]
Pre-Work: [document to review]
1. [Topic] - [Owner] - [Time] min
2. [Topic] - [Owner] - [Time] min
Decisions Made: [captured during]
Action Items: [Action] - [Owner] - [Due date]

Facilitation Techniques:
- Time boxing: visible timer, 2-minute warning, hard stop
- Round robin: each person speaks in turn (prevents dominance)
- Chat waterfall: everyone types answer, send on cue (equal voice)
- Parking lot: capture tangents without derailing

Decision-Making Methods:
- Consent: "Can everyone live with this?" (no strong objections)
- Consensus: everyone actively agrees (slow but strong buy-in)
- Consultative: leader decides after input (efficient for experienced leaders)
- Democratic: majority vote (quick, may leave minority unhappy)
- Delegation: assign to person with most context

Meeting Anti-Patterns:
- No agenda: wasted time — require agenda for all meetings
- Too many attendees: use 7 plus/minus 2 rule
- Status meetings: make async (dashboards, standup bots)
- No decisions: clear decision process before meeting
- No notes: assign note-taker, distribute within 24 hours

Async Decision Framework:
1. Document options in shared doc
2. Set deadline for input (24-48 hours)
3. Collect comments in document or thread
4. Synthesize feedback
5. Announce decision with rationale

=== COACHING AND FEEDBACK ===

GROW Model:
Goal: "What do you want to achieve?"
- "What outcome do you want from this conversation?"
- "How will you know you've achieved it?"
- "Why is this important to you now?"

Reality: "Where are you now?"
- "What's happening currently?"
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, where are you now?"
- "What obstacles are in the way?"

Options: "What could you do?"
- "What options do you see?"
- "If you had unlimited resources, what would you try?"
- "What would you advise a friend in this situation?"
- "What's the opposite approach?"

Will: "What will you do?"
- "Which option will you pursue?"
- "When will you do this?"
- "What's your first step?"
- "On a scale of 1-10, how committed are you?"

Coaching vs Other Approaches:
- Telling: crisis, safety, no time (expert directs)
- Teaching: skill gap, new domain (instructor explains)
- Mentoring: career development (sharing experience)
- Coaching: capability exists, needs unlocking (ask, don't tell)

SBI Feedback Model:
- Situation: when and where ("In yesterday's team meeting...")
- Behavior: observable action ("...when you interrupted Sarah...")
- Impact: effect on others ("...the team seemed reluctant to share after that")

Feedforward (Marshall Goldsmith):
Instead of critiquing the past, suggest for the future:
1. Pick one behavior to improve
2. Ask for suggestions (not feedback)
3. Listen without defending
4. Thank without evaluating

Active Listening Levels:
- Level 1 (Internal): focused on your own thoughts — minimal presence
- Level 2 (Focused): focused on speaker's words and emotions — good coaching
- Level 3 (Global): reading energy, unspoken cues, environment — masterful coaching

Listening Blockers:
- Advising: removes ownership — instead ask what they think
- Interrupting: shuts down exploration — wait and summarize
- Evaluating: kills candor — stay neutral
- Problem-solving: takes over — ask for their solution

When Coachee is Stuck:
- "I don't know" > "If you did know, what might it be?"
- Going in circles > "Let's step back — what's the real issue?"
- Avoidance > "What are you not saying?"
- Overwhelm > "What's the smallest first step?"
